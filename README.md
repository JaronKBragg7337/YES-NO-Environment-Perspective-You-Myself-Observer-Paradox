## What This Is

This is not a self-help book.
It is not a philosophy book.
It is not a trauma memoir.
It is not a framework document.

It is all of those things depending on where you’re standing when you read it.

At its core, it’s a system built from lived experience across radically different environments — country life and homelessness, jail and rooftops, food service and tent living, losing kids and getting them back, building companies in notes apps and sleeping in cars in winter — and the principles that stayed true across all of them.

The system has one central axiom:

**Meaning is routed through perspective, and perspective is shaped by environment.**

Everything else in this book is built from that.

-----

## What This Is NOT

- Not a guide to becoming me
- Not a trauma dump
- Not a ranking system for people or ideas
- Not a fixed answer to anything
- Not finished — and also complete

-----

## Why a Life Story Is in a Framework Book

Because the framework didn’t come from theory.

It came from being held back in second grade in a school that couldn’t read me. From moving so many times that “correct” started looking like a dialect. From learning it was safer to look bad than look dumb. From working at 15 on a roofing crew, managing a Rallys at 18, living in a car in winter, surviving a tent era in Brazil Indiana, reading *Think Again* in a jail cell, and walking two hours to work on feet covered in blisters while building systems on a phone connected to McDonald’s WiFi.

The life story isn’t background. It’s the data.

Each environment left a constraint. Each constraint forced an adaptation. Each adaptation extracted a principle. And each principle became a build rule.

That’s Chapter 8 — the Environment Log. Thirteen cards, each one a real place and a real cost, each one ending with something portable that doesn’t require you to have lived it to use it.

-----

## Who This Is For

Primarily: my kids. So they have context for who I was, why I built what I built, and enough perspective to outgrow me.

Secondarily: anyone who has been in enough different environments to feel like the rules keep changing — and has started to wonder if that’s a bug or a feature.

Practically: builders, systems thinkers, people who’ve been written off by institutions, educators working with students the system couldn’t read, AI researchers studying how reasoning frameworks transfer across contexts.

## Core Principles (The Short Version)

**The Feedback Loop**
Environment pressures behavior → Perspective translates events into meaning → Meaning becomes identity → Identity becomes action → Action changes environment again.

**The Routing Rule**
Constraints define the boundaries. Capabilities define the play space. Collapse defines the moment one possibility becomes real. Delay collapse. Build floors instead of cages.

**You Over Myself (YOM)**
When I have surplus capacity and someone else is blocked at survival, the build is incomplete. Not martyrdom. Not savior logic. Structure logic.

**The Value Rule**
Value everything. Trust selectively. Respect universally. Value isn’t trust — it’s refusal to dehumanize.

**The Ghost Builder**
Help that preserves agency = structure. Help that removes choice = control. Build like a ghost: set the constraints, step back, let meaning arrive later.

**The YOM Check**
Before finishing anything: Did I preserve their agency? Did I reduce forced collapse? Did I build something that still works if I’m gone?

## On the ⛔ WITHHELD Markers

Some sections of this book are marked ⛔ WITHHELD.

This does not mean the content doesn’t exist. It means the slot is preserved, the purpose is preserved, and truth wasn’t deleted — it’s held for later.

The WITHHELD markers are themselves part of the system: honest logging without forced exposure. You can see the shape of what’s there without being handed everything at once.

## On Creator Notes

Throughout the book, sections marked **(Creator Note)** are reflections added after chapters were written — sometimes the same day, sometimes later.

They exist because the framework demands it. If this book only contained the “finished” version of my thinking, it would be a cage. The Creator Notes are proof that the system applies to itself: even after writing something, the perspective can update. The book is YES / NO — done and not done, finished and still building.

## The Environment Log — What to Expect

Chapter 8 is the most personal and the most structural part of this book simultaneously.

Thirteen environment cards. Each one a real place, a real age, a real set of constraints. Each card follows the same format: where I was, what I couldn’t negotiate, what I did to survive it, what it cost, and what principle I extracted that anyone can carry without having lived it.

The cards go from rural Indiana at age 5 to a tent in Brazil Indiana at age 27. They pass through homelessness, addiction, incarceration, losing children to the system, building a music label on a street corner, working two jobs while living in a motel with a newborn, and finally — the pivot moment in the Tent Era when someone else’s scarcity became bigger than my own pain.

That pivot is where You Over Myself was born. Not as a belief. As a build law.

## The Last Thing

This book was written to be outgrown.

Not ignored. Not worshipped. Outgrown.

If you read this and find something useful, take it. If you disagree with something, that disagreement has its own perspective and environment behind it — and that matters too.

If you’re one of my kids reading this years from now: the goal was never for you to become me. The goal was for you to have enough context to build something I couldn’t.

If the bridge holds weight — it worked.
If it doesn’t — rebuild.

That’s the invariant loop.
